# Editorial Magazine

Designer: Alexey Brodovitch (Harper's Bazaar), Herb Lubalin, David Carson
Origin: Print editorial tradition — magazine layouts, newspaper typography, art direction
Tagline: "Every page is an editorial spread"

## Core Aesthetic

- Expressive display typography as the primary design element
- Dramatic scale contrast between massive headlines and small body text
- Editorial grid with intentional, purposeful breaks
- Photography-forward with art-directed section layouts
- Narrative flow — the page tells a story as you scroll
- Bold color accent or stark black-and-white, never both at once

## Typography

### Headlines
- Expressive serif (Canela, Domaine Display, Freight Big, Lyon Display)
- Extreme weights — ultra-light OR ultra-bold, never medium
- Mixed with condensed grotesque for contrast
- Generous letter-spacing on light cuts, tight or overlapping on bold cuts

### Body Text
- Classic text serif (Freight Text, Tiempos Text, Caslon) or neutral grotesque (Neue Haas Grotesk)
- Small, restrained — body text is not the star
- High line-height for readability within dense layouts

### Technical/Accent
- Uppercase grotesque for labels, captions, section markers
- Small caps for bylines, datelines, issue numbers
- Tracked wide — often 0.1–0.2em letter-spacing

### Hierarchy
- Three or more distinct size steps with extreme contrast (e.g. 120px headline, 14px body)
- Pull quotes at 40–60px break visual rhythm
- Drop caps or large first letters signal editorial prestige

## Layout Patterns

- Multi-column grid that gets deliberately violated — text runs into image zones
- Full-bleed photography with type overlaid, reversed, or inset
- Asymmetric compositions: one massive element anchors, everything else is small
- Sidebar columns for secondary content, captions, commentary — feels like a magazine gutter
- Section breaks read as chapter openers: full-width, typographically bold, minimal imagery
- Horizontal scroll or stacked panels create page-turn rhythm on the web

## Signature Elements

- Enormous display type that bleeds off the canvas edge
- Pull quotes styled larger than the headline with italic or contrasting weight
- Drop caps in contrasting color or oversized serif
- Thin rule lines separating columns and sections (0.5–1px, not borders)
- Dateline or issue number in small caps at top of page (e.g. "VOL. 03 — SPRING 2025")
- Photo credit and caption typography — small, precise, never neglected
- Running headers or footers tracking section context
- Negative-space compositions where white space is structural, not padding
- Orphaned single words at large scale as decorative typographic objects
- Deliberate column overflow — text intentionally runs behind images

## Textures & Effects

- Paper grain or subtle halftone on backgrounds to evoke print
- High-contrast duotone photography (one ink color over black)
- Ink bleed simulation: slight rough edges on large letterforms
- Overprint effects — type or color blocks that "print" over photos with multiply blending

## When to Use

- Editorial sites, online magazines, journalism platforms
- Long-form content products (newsletters, essays, reports)
- Cultural brands — art, fashion, architecture, film
- Agency sites that want to signal taste and craft
- Book publishers and literary brands
- Annual reports designed to be read, not scanned
- Brand storytelling with rich written content
- Award submissions and case study presentations

## When NOT to Use

- SaaS dashboards or data-heavy tools
- E-commerce with large product catalogs (kills browse flow)
- Mobile-first apps (extreme scale contrast breaks at small screens)
- B2B enterprise software (too expressive for procurement buyers)
- Anything requiring fast information retrieval
